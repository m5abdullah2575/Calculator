BMR Calculation Methods & Formulas

Our calorie calculator uses three scientifically proven formulas to calculate your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR). Each formula has its own strengths and accuracy for different populations.

1. Mifflin-St Jeor Equation (Most Accurate - Default)

Developed in 1990, considered the most accurate for the general population.

For Men: BMR = 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) - 5 × age + 5

For Women: BMR = 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) - 5 × age - 161

Best for: Most people, general population accuracy

2. Revised Harris-Benedict Equation

Updated version of the original 1919 Harris-Benedict equation, revised in 1984.

For Men: BMR = 13.397W + 4.799H - 5.677A + 88.362

For Women: BMR = 9.247W + 3.098H - 4.330A + 447.593

Best for: Historical comparison, widely recognized formula

3. Katch-McArdle Formula

Uses lean body mass instead of total body weight for more accurate results.

Formula: BMR = 370 + 21.6 × (1 - F/100) × W

Where F = body fat percentage, W = weight in kg

Best for: People who know their body fat percentage, athletes, lean individuals

Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE)

Formula: TDEE = BMR × Activity Factor

TDEE represents the total calories you burn in a day including exercise, work, and daily activities.

Activity Factors:

  • • BMR Only: 1.0 (basal metabolic rate - calories at complete rest)
  • • Sedentary: 1.2 (little/no exercise, desk job)
  • • Light Activity: 1.375 (light exercise 1-3 times/week)
  • • Moderate Activity: 1.55 (moderate exercise 4-5 times/week)
  • • Active: 1.725 (daily exercise or intense exercise 3-4 times/week)
  • • Very Active: 1.9 (intense exercise 6-7 times/week)
  • • Extra Active: 1.9 (very intense exercise daily, physical job)

Complete Guide to Calorie-Based Weight Management

Understanding Calorie Deficit and Surplus

The 3,500 Calorie Rule: 1 pound of body fat equals approximately 3,500 calories.

Weight Loss: Create a calorie deficit by eating fewer calories than you burn.

Weight Gain: Create a calorie surplus by eating more calories than you burn.

Maintenance: Eat approximately the same number of calories you burn daily.

Safe Weight Loss Guidelines

  • • Moderate Loss: 1-2 pounds per week (500-1000 calorie daily deficit)
  • • Minimum Calories: Never go below 1,200 cal/day (women) or 1,500 cal/day (men)
  • • Sustainable Rate: 1% of body weight per week maximum
  • • Muscle Preservation: Include strength training to maintain lean mass
  • • Nutrition Quality: Focus on nutrient-dense, whole foods

Zigzag Calorie Cycling Method

Zigzag cycling involves alternating between higher and lower calorie days to prevent metabolic adaptation.

Example Weekly Plan:

  • • Monday: -500 calories (low day)
  • • Tuesday: -300 calories (moderate day)
  • • Wednesday: -700 calories (very low day)
  • • Thursday: -300 calories (moderate day)
  • • Friday: -500 calories (low day)
  • • Saturday: +100 calories (refeed day)
  • • Sunday: -300 calories (moderate day)

Benefits: Prevents metabolic slowdown, improves adherence, maintains hormone levels

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

How accurate are calorie calculators?

Calorie calculators provide estimates based on scientific formulas. Individual variations in metabolism, body composition, and genetics can affect actual calorie needs by 10-20%. Use results as a starting point and adjust based on real-world results.

Which BMR formula should I use?

Mifflin-St Jeor is most accurate for the general population. Use Katch-McArdle if you know your body fat percentage and are relatively lean. Harris-Benedict is the oldest but still widely used formula.

How often should I recalculate my calories?

Recalculate every 10-15 pounds of weight loss/gain, or every 3-4 months. Age and changes in activity level also warrant recalculation.

Can I eat below my BMR?

Eating significantly below BMR for extended periods can slow metabolism and cause muscle loss. Generally not recommended except under medical supervision.

Why am I not losing weight despite a calorie deficit?

Common reasons include: underestimating food intake, overestimating exercise calories, water retention, metabolic adaptation, or medical conditions. Track consistently for 2-3 weeks before making adjustments.
