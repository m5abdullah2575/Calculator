Understanding MET Values & Calorie Burn

What are MET Values?

MET (Metabolic Equivalent of Task) is a physiological measure expressing the energy cost of physical activities.

Formula:

Calories = (MET × Weight in kg × Time in hours)

  • • 1 MET = energy used at rest (sitting quietly)
  • • 3-6 METs = light to moderate activities
  • • 6+ METs = vigorous activities
  • • Based on: healthy 40-year-old male, 70kg

Activity Intensity Levels

Light Intensity (2-4 METs)

Examples: Walking slowly, light housework, stretching

• Walking 2-3 mph: 2.5-3.5 METs

• Light cleaning: 2.5 METs

• Yoga: 3.0 METs

Moderate Intensity (4-6 METs)

Examples: Brisk walking, cycling, dancing

• Walking 3.5-4 mph: 4.0-5.0 METs

• Cycling 10-12 mph: 6.0 METs

• Dancing: 4.5-5.0 METs

Vigorous Intensity (6+ METs)

Examples: Running, swimming, sports

• Running 6 mph: 9.8 METs

• Swimming laps: 8.0 METs

• Basketball: 8.0 METs

Factors Affecting Calorie Burn

Body Mass & Composition

  • • Weight: Heavier people burn more calories
  • • Muscle mass: More muscle = higher burn rate
  • • Body fat: Less metabolically active
  • • Height: Taller people generally burn more

Age & Fitness Level

  • • Age: Metabolism slows with age
  • • Fitness level: Fitter people are more efficient
  • • Training: Adaptation reduces calorie burn
  • • Recovery: Affects next workout intensity

Environmental Factors

  • • Temperature: Heat increases calorie burn
  • • Altitude: Higher altitude = more effort
  • • Terrain: Hills and rough terrain increase burn
  • • Weather: Wind and conditions affect intensity

Exercise Intensity

  • • Heart rate: Higher HR = more calories
  • • Pace: Faster pace increases burn rate
  • • Resistance: Added weight/resistance
  • • Intervals: High-intensity bursts

Lifestyle Factors

  • • Sleep: Poor sleep reduces metabolism
  • • Diet: Affects metabolic rate
  • • Stress: Chronic stress impacts burn rate
  • • Hydration: Dehydration reduces efficiency

Activity-Specific

  • • Technique: Efficient form reduces burn
  • • Equipment: Weight of gear/equipment
  • • Surface: Soft vs. hard surfaces
  • • Duration: Fatigue affects efficiency

Tips for Maximizing Calorie Burn

Effective Strategies

  • • Increase intensity: Add intervals, resistance, or speed
  • • Compound movements: Multi-muscle exercises burn more
  • • Add incline: Hills or treadmill incline increase effort
  • • Extend duration: Longer sessions burn more total calories
  • • Mix activities: Cross-training prevents adaptation
  • • Stay hydrated: Proper hydration maintains efficiency
  • • Consistent schedule: Regular exercise boosts metabolism

Advanced Techniques

  • • HIIT training: High-intensity intervals for maximum burn
  • • Circuit training: Minimal rest between exercises
  • • Supersets: Back-to-back exercises without rest
  • • Plyometrics: Explosive movements increase intensity
  • • Functional training: Real-world movement patterns
  • • Progressive overload: Gradually increase difficulty
  • • Active recovery: Light activity on rest days

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate are calories burned calculations?

Calorie calculators provide estimates based on average MET values. Individual variations in metabolism, fitness level, and body composition can affect actual calorie burn by ±20-30%. Use results as a general guide and adjust based on your specific goals and progress.

Why do heavier people burn more calories?

Heavier individuals require more energy to move their body mass. Like carrying a heavier backpack, more weight means more work for your muscles and cardiovascular system, resulting in higher calorie expenditure for the same activity.

Should I focus on duration or intensity for weight loss?

Both matter! For fat loss, moderate-intensity longer duration activities (45-60 min) burn more fat as fuel. High-intensity shorter workouts (20-30 min) burn more calories per minute and boost metabolism post-exercise. A combination approach is most effective.

Do I burn calories after exercise ends?

Yes! This is called EPOC (Excess Post-Exercise Oxygen Consumption) or "afterburn effect." High-intensity workouts can increase your metabolic rate for up to 24 hours post-exercise, burning additional calories as your body recovers.

How does muscle mass affect calorie burn?

Muscle tissue is metabolically active, burning calories even at rest. Each pound of muscle burns approximately 6-7 calories per day at rest, while fat burns only 2-3 calories. Building muscle through strength training increases your daily calorie burn.
