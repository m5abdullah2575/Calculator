Zone 1: Active Recovery (50-60% HRR)

Zone 2: Base Training (60-70% HRR)
Purpose: Aerobic base building, fat burning, endurance foundation

Zone 3: Aerobic Development (70-80% HRR)
Purpose: Moderate intensity, endurance building, aerobic power
Develop aerobic capacity and endurance. Conversation becomes more difficult but still possible in short phrases.
Zone 4: Lactate Threshold (80-90% HRR)

Train your lactate threshold and improve high-intensity endurance. Conversation is very limited to single words.

Purpose: Maximum effort, anaerobic power, speed development
Develop neuromuscular power and speed. This is maximum effort training that can only be sustained for short periods.

Understanding Target Heart Rate

What is Target Heart Rate?

Target heart rate is the ideal range of heart beats per minute (bpm) during exercise to maximize cardiovascular benefits while avoiding overexertion. Training within specific heart rate zones ensures you're working at the right intensity for your fitness goals, whether that's fat burning, endurance building, or performance improvement.

Key Heart Rate Concepts

Maximum Heart Rate (MHR)

The highest number of beats per minute your heart can achieve during intense exercise. Most accurately measured through stress testing, but commonly estimated using age-based formulas.

Resting Heart Rate (RHR)

Your heart rate when completely at rest, typically measured first thing in the morning. Normal range is 50-100 bpm, with athletes often having lower resting rates.

Heart Rate Reserve (HRR)

The difference between your maximum and resting heart rate (MHR - RHR). Used in the Karvonen method for more personalized training zone calculations.

Training Zones

Specific heart rate ranges that target different physiological adaptations, from active recovery to maximum anaerobic power development.

Maximum Heart Rate Formulas

Haskell & Fox (1971)

Formula: 220 - age

The most widely used formula, simple and straightforward. Good for general population estimates.

Tanaka, Monahan & Seals (2001)

Formula: 208 - (0.7 × age)

Based on more recent research with larger, more diverse populations. Often more accurate than the traditional formula.

Nes, Janszky, Wisloff, Stoylen, Karlsen (2013)

Formula: 211 - (0.64 × age)

The most recent formula based on large-scale studies. May be most accurate for athletic populations.

Training Applications by Heart Rate Zone

Zone 1 & 2: Aerobic Base Building

  • Long runs/rides: Build aerobic capacity and endurance
  • Recovery sessions: Active recovery between hard workouts
  • Fat burning: Optimal zone for weight loss goals
  • Base fitness: Foundation for all other training

Zone 3, 4 & 5: Performance Training

  • Tempo workouts: Sustain race pace efforts
  • Interval training: High-intensity repeated efforts
  • Lactate threshold: Improve sustainable high intensity
  • VO2 max development: Maximum aerobic power

Training Balance is Key

Most of your training (80%) should be in Zones 1-2 for aerobic base building, with only 20% in higher intensity zones. This 80/20 rule helps prevent overtraining and reduces injury risk while maximizing adaptations.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate are age-based heart rate formulas?

Age-based formulas provide reasonable estimates for most people, but individual variation can be significant (±10-15 bpm). For the most accurate training zones, consider getting a maximum heart rate test performed by a qualified professional, especially if you're a serious athlete.

Should I always include resting heart rate in calculations?

Yes, if you know your resting heart rate. The Karvonen method (using heart rate reserve) provides more personalized training zones than simple percentage-based methods. Measure your resting HR first thing in the morning for best accuracy.

Which maximum heart rate formula is most accurate?

The Tanaka formula (208 - 0.7 × age) and Nes formula (211 - 0.64 × age) are based on more recent research and may be more accurate than the traditional Haskell & Fox formula, especially for older adults and athletes. However, individual testing remains the gold standard.

How do I measure my heart rate during exercise?

Use a chest strap heart rate monitor for most accuracy, or a quality wrist-based fitness tracker. Smartphone apps and manual pulse checking are less reliable during exercise. Take periodic checks to ensure you're in your target zone.

Can medications affect my target heart rate?

Yes, medications like beta-blockers can significantly lower both resting and maximum heart rate. If you take heart medications, consult with your doctor about appropriate exercise intensity targets, as standard formulas may not apply.
